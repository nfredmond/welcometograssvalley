Biography & Life Story

Julia Park Tracey is a Grass Valley resident, award-winning journalist with a 40-year career, and author of nine books who specializes in resurrecting the stories of her female ancestors. Her newest novel, Whoa, Nelly!, draws on a lifelong obsession with Laura Ingalls Wilder that began when she received the Little House series on her fourth birthday‚Äîbooks she still owns and rereads every other year.

Early Life & Education

Julia grew up in Penngrove, California (Sonoma County) as one of five siblings. Two childhood discoveries shaped her path: the Little House on the Prairie books at age 4, and discovering Patti Smith's poetry as a teenager‚Äîlearning she could "write about being angry."

She won the Press Democrat's High School Journalism Award two years running, then earned a BA in Journalism from San Francisco State University and an MA in English from CSU East Bay (her thesis became her first novel).

The Road to Grass Valley

In February 2019, Julia lost her 21-year-old son Austin to suicide. "My life just stopped. I did not write for two years." In 2021, she and her husband Patrick moved to Grass Valley and bought a "battered Victorian home." Restoring it themselves‚Äîhammering nails, laying tile‚Äîbroke her writer's block and launched her most prolific creative period.

Today she describes herself as a "bestselling author, poet laureate emeritus, cat lady, chicken wrangler, bee-witch, bird-nerd, word weirdo, and history stan."

Whoa, Nelly! A Love Story (With Footnotes)

Nelly is a 45-year-old librarian whose life has been shaped by her obsession with Laura Ingalls Wilder's Little House books. When she loses her job, she embarks on a train pilgrimage to De Smet, South Dakota, joining a literary tour of fellow fans‚Äîand discovers both the complexities of Wilder's legacy and unexpected love.

Publisher: Sibylline Press Format: Trade paperback ($18) Pages: 238
"A funny, fierce, nostalgic, and deeply tender novel... The narrative is by turns laugh-out-loud funny and heartbreakingly honest." ‚Äî Kirkus Reviews (Starred Review)

The Footnote Innovation

The novel uses footnotes as a narrative device‚ÄîNelly directly addresses readers about Wilder, history, and her own life. Kirkus notes: "The footnotes could be gimmicky, but they work effectively here to offer clever insights into Wilder and her work‚Äîand into Nelly's life as well."

Autobiographical Elements

Julia admits Nelly is her "literary doppelganger." She's read the Little House novels "every year for 50 years at least." At age 13, she participated in a 4H Wagon Trek‚Äîdressed as a pioneer girl for four days, riding 50 miles in covered wagons. She wrote this novel on an actual cross-country train during her Doris Diaries book tour.
